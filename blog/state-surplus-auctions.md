Most people who know about government auctions think federal — GSA Auctions, military surplus, GovDeals. But every U.S. state runs its own surplus property program, and these are some of the most underutilized deals in government auctions.

State surplus means less national competition, items located within your state, and a steady supply of well-maintained fleet vehicles, university lab equipment, DOT machinery, and office furniture. If you're not watching your state's program, you're leaving money on the table.

## How State Programs Work (and How They Differ from Federal)

Federal surplus is managed centrally by GSA. State surplus is managed individually — each state has its own agency, its own rules, its own platform, and its own quirks.

**Key differences from federal auctions:**

- **Selling agency**: State Department of Administration, General Services, or a similarly named agency manages surplus disposal. Sometimes it's delegated to individual departments (DOT, universities, state police).
- **Platforms**: Some states run their own auction websites. Others contract with GovDeals, PublicSurplus, or Municibid. A few still hold live, in-person auctions.
- **Who can buy**: Generally open to the public. Some states give priority to other government agencies or nonprofits before opening items to the public.
- **Buyer's premiums**: Varies. States using GovDeals will carry the platform's premium. States running their own sales may charge nothing.
- **Inventory type**: State surplus reflects what state governments actually use — which is different from federal. Far more fleet vehicles, DOT equipment, and university assets. Less military and defense-related equipment.

## Notable State Surplus Programs

Some states run particularly active or well-organized programs. Here are a few worth knowing:

### Texas — TxSmartBuy / Texas Facilities Commission

Texas is the gold standard for state surplus. The Texas Facilities Commission manages a massive surplus program with a dedicated warehouse in Austin. Inventory includes state fleet vehicles (Texas has one of the largest state fleets in the country), office equipment, IT hardware, and industrial tools.

Texas uses a mix of online auctions through their own portal and periodic live auctions. The state also sells through GovDeals for certain categories. Fleet vehicles — particularly Crown Victorias, Ford F-150s, and Chevy Tahoes from state law enforcement — are consistently popular.

### California — Department of General Services (DGS)

California's surplus program is huge, reflecting the size of the state government. The DGS Office of Fleet and Asset Management handles vehicle sales, while other surplus goes through multiple channels including public auction events and online listings.

CalTrans (the state DOT) is a major source of heavy equipment: dump trucks, sweepers, excavators, and traffic management equipment. University of California and Cal State systems contribute lab equipment, IT hardware, and furniture. Given California's size, items are spread across the state — check locations carefully.

### Florida — Department of Management Services

Florida runs its surplus through the Division of Surplus Property within the Department of Management Services. The state has a central surplus warehouse in Tallahassee and sells through a combination of their own system and GovDeals.

Florida's warm climate means less corrosion on vehicles and equipment — a legitimate advantage over northern state surplus. Fleet vehicles, marine equipment (Florida has a lot of waterway management), and university surplus are the highlights.

### New York — Office of General Services (OGS)

New York State OGS runs regular surplus auctions covering everything from state police vehicles to DOT snowplows. The state uses a mix of live auctions (often held at the state fairgrounds in Syracuse) and online listings.

DOT equipment is the star here — New York maintains an enormous fleet of snowplows, salt spreaders, graders, and heavy trucks for its harsh winters. When this equipment is rotated out, it's available at surplus. Buyers from milder-climate states sometimes buy New York snowplows specifically for the heavy-duty truck chassis.

### Other States Worth Watching

- **Georgia**: Active online surplus through the Department of Administrative Services. Strong vehicle inventory.
- **Ohio**: Uses GovDeals for most surplus. ODOT equipment is a regular draw.
- **Virginia**: Close proximity to D.C. means a blend of state and quasi-federal surplus. VDOT equipment is plentiful.
- **Michigan**: MDOT sells heavy equipment in regular cycles. Great for snowplow and salt truck buyers.
- **Arizona**: Dry climate means rust-free vehicles and equipment — a significant selling point for resellers.

## What Do States Sell?

**Fleet vehicles** — This is the highest-volume category across virtually every state. Police cruisers, administrative sedans, pickup trucks, vans, and SUVs. State fleet vehicles are maintained on schedule (the state keeps records) and replaced on mileage or age cycles. Common models: Ford Explorer (police), Dodge Charger (state police), Ford F-150/F-250 (DOT and parks), Chevy Tahoe (various agencies).

**DOT equipment** — Dump trucks, snowplows, motor graders, mowers, line stripers, message boards, traffic signals, arrow boards, road signs. State DOTs are the largest equipment operators in many states and rotate equipment on 10-20 year cycles.

**University and school surplus** — Lab equipment, classroom furniture, computers, audio/visual gear, cafeteria equipment, even dormitory furniture. State universities generate a constant stream of surplus as they renovate buildings and upgrade technology.

**Office equipment and furniture** — Desks, chairs, cubicles, printers, copiers, phones. Government offices refresh on long cycles, so the furniture is often commercial-grade and still has years of life.

**Specialized equipment** — Forestry equipment, agricultural testing instruments, water treatment components, medical equipment from state hospitals. Niche, but high-value for the right buyer.

## How to Find Your State's Surplus Program

**Step 1**: Search "[your state] surplus property" or "[your state] state auction." The managing agency's page should be in the top results.

**Step 2**: Check if the state uses a third-party platform. Many states list through GovDeals, PublicSurplus, or Municibid in addition to (or instead of) their own sites. If the state site looks outdated or limited, check GovDeals and filter by your state — you'll likely find more listings there.

**Step 3**: Sign up for email notifications. Most state programs and third-party platforms let you set alerts for categories and locations. Set one for your state and your preferred categories (vehicles, equipment, IT, etc.).

**Step 4**: Look for the surplus warehouse. Many states have a physical location where smaller items are stored and available for in-person inspection. Texas, Florida, Georgia, and several others operate central surplus warehouses that are open to the public on set days.

## Advantages of State Surplus Auctions

**Less competition.** Federal auctions on GSA and GovDeals attract buyers nationwide. State surplus — especially on state-run platforms — draws a mostly local audience. Fewer bidders means lower closing prices.

**Local pickup.** Items are located within your state, usually within driving distance. No cross-country shipping calculations, no $5,000 freight bills. Drive there with a truck and trailer and drive home with your purchase.

**Consistent inventory.** State agencies dispose of surplus on predictable cycles — end of fiscal year (June 30 for most states), fleet rotation schedules, building renovations. Once you learn the pattern, you can anticipate what's coming.

**Better condition transparency.** State fleet programs typically maintain detailed service records. When you buy a state surplus vehicle, the agency can often provide maintenance history going back years. That's rare in the private used market.

**No dealer middlemen.** You're buying from the government directly (or through their contracted auction platform). There's no dealer markup, no lot fees, and no negotiation games.

## Tips for State Surplus Buyers

1. **Check multiple channels.** Your state might list some items on their own site, some on GovDeals, and hold live auctions for others. Monitor all channels.
2. **Watch fiscal year timing.** Many agencies push surplus out before their fiscal year ends (June 30 for most states, September 30 for a few). Volume spikes in May-June.
3. **Start with vehicles.** State fleet vehicles are the most liquid category — easy to evaluate, easy to resell, and plentiful. Good starting point for new buyers.
4. **Build relationships.** State surplus warehouse staff are often the same people year after year. Being a reliable, on-time buyer who doesn't cause problems gets you information — like when a big lot is about to be listed.
