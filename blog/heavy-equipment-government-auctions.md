A used CAT 320 excavator retails for $80,000-$150,000 from a dealer. The same machine at a government auction? $25,000-$60,000 is common. Government agencies don't haggle, don't negotiate trade-ins, and don't care about maximizing resale — they just need the equipment gone. That's your opportunity.

## Where to Find Heavy Equipment at Government Auctions

Not all auction platforms carry heavy equipment. Here's where to look:

**GovPlanet** — The biggest player in government heavy equipment. Run by Ritchie Bros (the world's largest industrial auctioneer), GovPlanet specializes in military and government surplus equipment. Expect excavators, wheel loaders, dozers, generators, and tactical vehicles. Buyer's premium: 10-15%.

**IronPlanet** — GovPlanet's sister site, also owned by Ritchie Bros. More commercial and municipal equipment here, less military. Their IronClad Assurance program provides equipment condition reports with guaranteed descriptions. Buyer's premium: 10-15%.

**GovDeals** — The largest general government surplus platform. Heavy equipment from municipalities, counties, school districts, and state DOTs shows up regularly. Less specialized than GovPlanet, but you'll find gems — especially from small towns that don't attract heavy bidding traffic. Buyer's premium: 7.5-12.5%.

**GSA Auctions** — Federal surplus only. Military engineering equipment, USDA forestry machines, DOE facility equipment. Inventory is less predictable but the zero buyer's premium makes it worth monitoring.

**State surplus programs** — Many states sell DOT equipment directly. Snowplows, graders, dump trucks, mowers — the bread and butter of state highway departments. Often sold through dedicated state auction sites or through GovDeals.

## What Types of Equipment Are Available?

Government agencies at every level use heavy equipment. Here's what regularly comes to market:

- **Excavators**: Hydraulic excavators from CAT, John Deere, Komatsu, Case. Municipal utility work and military construction units generate steady supply.
- **Wheel loaders**: Commonly used by DOTs for snow removal, material handling, and road maintenance. CAT 930/950 series are frequent listings.
- **Bulldozers**: Military and DOT surplus. CAT D6 and D7 models appear regularly, often with low hours for their age.
- **Motor graders**: State DOTs are the primary source. Expect CAT 12H/140 series and John Deere 772 models.
- **Dump trucks**: Single-axle and tandem from county road departments. High mileage but built for punishment.
- **Backhoe loaders**: CAT 420/430, John Deere 310/410. Every municipality owns one and replaces them on 10-15 year cycles.
- **Generators**: Diesel generators from 10kW portables to 500kW+ stationary units. Military surplus generators are particularly common.
- **Forklifts**: Warehouses on military bases and government facilities cycle through forklifts regularly.

## Inspection Is Non-Negotiable

This is the single most important piece of advice for buying heavy equipment at auction: **inspect before you bid.** Not "look at the photos." Not "read the description." Physically go to the facility and put your eyes and hands on the machine.

Here's why:

- Auction descriptions are written by clerks, not mechanics. "Runs and drives" might mean "started once last month."
- Photos often don't show undercarriage wear, hydraulic leaks, or frame cracks.
- Hour meters can be broken, replaced, or rolled back. Visual wear tells the real story.
- A machine that "needs minor repair" could need a $15,000 engine rebuild.

Most government auction listings include an inspection window (typically 2-5 days before the auction closes) and a contact phone number for the selling agency. Call ahead. Schedule a time. Bring someone who knows equipment if you don't.

If you can't inspect in person, IronPlanet's IronClad reports are the next best thing — they provide independent condition assessments with guaranteed accuracy.

## Transport Logistics and Costs

Heavy equipment doesn't fit in a pickup truck. Plan your transport before you bid, not after you win.

**Cost estimates for hauling:**
- Local (under 100 miles): $500-$1,500 for a standard lowboy trailer load
- Regional (100-500 miles): $1,500-$4,000
- Cross-country (1,000+ miles): $4,000-$10,000+

Factors that affect cost: equipment weight, dimensions (over-width/over-height permits add cost), origin/destination accessibility, and seasonal demand. Moving a D6 dozer from Texas to Michigan in winter will cost more than a backhoe from one county to the next.

**Options for transport:**
- **Hire a heavy haul company**: Get 3 quotes minimum. uShip and HaulHub are good starting points.
- **Rent a trailer**: If you have a CDL and a truck rated for the weight, renting a lowboy trailer runs $200-$500/day.
- **Drive it**: Some equipment (wheel loaders, graders) can be driven short distances on public roads with proper permits and escort vehicles. Check your state's regulations.

Always add transport cost to your maximum bid calculation. A $30,000 excavator with $6,000 in shipping is a $36,000 excavator.

Buyer's premiums hit harder on big-ticket items. Here's what the math looks like on a $40,000 winning bid:

| Platform | Premium | Total Cost |

## Who's Buying?

Government equipment auctions attract a specific mix of buyers:

- **Small to mid-size contractors**: The primary audience. They need working machines at below-dealer prices and have the mechanical knowledge to evaluate condition.
- **Farmers and ranchers**: Loaders, backhoes, and dump trucks are ranch staples. Government surplus offers better value than the dealer lot.
- **Municipalities buying from other municipalities**: Smaller towns often buy surplus from larger cities or state DOTs. A city retiring a 2015 grader is selling to a township that's still running a 2002 model.
- **Exporters**: Used American equipment is in demand in Central America, Africa, and Southeast Asia. Export buyers are common at military surplus auctions.
- **Parts dealers**: Non-running equipment sells for component value. A blown-engine excavator still has a valuable hydraulic pump, boom, and undercarriage.

## Tips for Buying Smart

1. **Know the market value before you bid.** Check Ritchie Bros' auction results archive, MachineryTrader, and Equipment Watch for comparable recent sales.
2. **Budget 10-20% of purchase price for immediate maintenance.** Filters, fluids, belts, hoses. Government maintenance is usually by-the-book but deferred at end of life.
3. **Check for liens and clear title.** Government sales should always come with clear title, but verify the paperwork before you haul anything away.
4. **Start with smaller equipment.** A backhoe or skid steer is a lower-risk first purchase than a $75,000 excavator. Learn the process on a $5,000 item.
