If you're buying government surplus, you've probably landed on GovDeals and PublicSurplus. They look similar — both host auctions for state and local government agencies selling off used equipment, vehicles, and supplies. But the differences between them matter, especially if you're buying regularly or reselling for profit.

Here's a direct comparison.

## The Big Difference: Buyer's Premiums

This is the single most important distinction and the one that affects your wallet directly.

- **GovDeals**: 12.5% buyer's premium on most auctions. A $1,000 winning bid costs you $1,125.
- **PublicSurplus**: Usually 0%. Some sellers set a small premium (up to 5%), but many charge nothing.

On a $500 item, that's the difference between paying $500 and paying $562.50. Over a year of regular buying, the savings on PublicSurplus add up fast.

Why the difference? GovDeals is owned by Liquidity Services, a publicly traded company. The buyer's premium is their primary revenue model. PublicSurplus charges fees to the selling agencies instead, which means buyers typically pay less.

## Inventory and Selection

**GovDeals** has the larger inventory by a wide margin. Thousands of active auctions at any given time covering vehicles, heavy equipment, IT equipment, office furniture, industrial tools, and more. If you're looking for something specific — a particular type of truck, a specific piece of construction equipment — GovDeals is more likely to have it.

**PublicSurplus** has a smaller but still substantial catalog. The inventory tends to lean more toward everyday municipal surplus: office furniture, computers, police equipment, parks department tools, and fleet vehicles. You'll find less heavy construction equipment and military surplus compared to GovDeals.

The real difference is depth. GovDeals has more of everything. But "more" also means more bidders, which drives prices up.

## Seller Types

Both platforms serve government agencies, but the mix is different.

**GovDeals** works with a broader range of sellers: cities, counties, school districts, state agencies, utilities, transit authorities, and even some quasi-governmental organizations. They've aggressively signed up agencies nationwide, which is why their inventory is larger.

**PublicSurplus** has strong coverage among cities, counties, and school districts, particularly in the western and midwestern United States. Their seller base is slightly more concentrated among smaller municipalities.

## Geographic Coverage

**GovDeals** has strong national coverage. You'll find listings in all 50 states, with particularly dense coverage in the Southeast and Texas.

**PublicSurplus** also covers all 50 states but has historically been stronger in the West — California, Oregon, Washington, Colorado, and Arizona are well-represented. Coverage in the Northeast and Southeast is thinner compared to GovDeals.

If you're only interested in buying from agencies within driving distance, check which platform has more sellers in your region. This can matter more than overall inventory size.

## Search and Usability

Neither platform will win any design awards, but they differ in usability.

**GovDeals** has a functional search with category filters, location filters, and keyword search. It works, but the interface feels dated. Auction listings vary in quality — some have detailed descriptions and a dozen photos, others have a one-line description and a single blurry image. GovDeals also sends email alerts for saved searches, which is useful.

**PublicSurplus** has a cleaner, slightly more modern interface. Search works well with keyword and category filters. Listings tend to have reasonable descriptions. The site is generally easier to navigate for first-time users.

That said, both platforms have search limitations. You can't easily compare across both simultaneously, sorting is basic, and there's no way to filter by true cost (bid price plus premium).

## Payment and Pickup

The process is nearly identical on both platforms:

- **Payment**: Due within 5 business days of winning. Both accept credit cards, wire transfers, and cashier's checks. GovDeals sellers sometimes add a credit card processing fee (2-3%) on top of the buyer's premium.
- **Pickup**: Buyer is responsible for removal, typically within 10-15 business days. Pickup is at the government agency's location during their business hours.
- **Condition**: Everything is sold as-is, where-is. No returns, no warranties.

No meaningful advantage to either platform here.

## Which Is Better for Different Use Cases

**You're a reseller focused on margins**: PublicSurplus. The 0% buyer's premium means more profit per item. If the same item sells for the same bid price on both platforms, PublicSurplus nets you 12.5% more margin.

**You're looking for a specific vehicle or piece of equipment**: GovDeals. Larger inventory means a higher chance of finding what you need. The premium is worth it if the alternative is not finding the item at all.

**You're buying office furniture or IT equipment**: Check both. These categories are well-represented on both platforms. Start with PublicSurplus for the fee savings, then check GovDeals for anything you didn't find.

**You're in the western US**: PublicSurplus may have more local options, saving you on transport costs.

**You're buying heavy equipment**: GovDeals has significantly more construction and industrial equipment. PublicSurplus has some, but the selection is thinner.

## The Verdict

There's no single winner. GovDeals has more inventory and broader geographic coverage. PublicSurplus has better fees and is friendlier to your bottom line.

The smart play is to search both. The same type of item can appear on either platform, and the price difference (after fees) can be significant. A $2,000 winning bid on GovDeals costs $2,250 after the premium. The same item at $2,200 on PublicSurplus costs $2,200 flat — and that's $50 in your pocket.
