Government surplus auctions are one of the best-kept secrets for finding deals on vehicles, electronics, heavy equipment, and more. Federal, state, and local agencies sell billions of dollars worth of surplus property every year — often at a fraction of retail price.

But if you go in blind, you can get burned. Here are five tips to make sure you come out ahead.

## 1. Always Calculate the True Cost

The bid price is not your final price. Different platforms charge different buyer's premiums:

| Platform | Buyer's Premium |
|----------|----------------|
| GSA Auctions | 0% (no fee) |
| PublicSurplus | Varies by seller (usually 0%) |
| Municibid | 9% |
| GovDeals | 7.5-12.5% |

A $1,000 winning bid on GovDeals could cost you $1,125 after the 12.5% premium. Factor this in *before* you bid, not after.

**Pro tip:** GSA Auctions charges no buyer's premium at all. The same type of item might be cheaper on GSA even if the bid price is slightly higher.

## 2. Inspect Before You Bid (If Possible)

Government auction listings are notorious for vague descriptions. "Lot of electronics" could mean 48 working laptops or 48 broken monitors. Photos are often minimal and taken in poor lighting.

Most government surplus auctions allow in-person inspection before the auction closes. Take advantage of this:

- Call the contact person listed on the auction
- Ask for additional photos
- Ask about the item's service history (government vehicles are usually well-documented)
- Look for "as-is" disclaimers — they mean exactly that

## 3. Factor in Pickup and Logistics

This is where most beginners get caught. Nearly all government surplus is **pickup only** — the agency won't ship it to you. And you typically have 10-15 business days to remove it.

Before you bid, know:
- How far is the pickup location?
- Do you need a truck, trailer, or flatbed?
- Will you need to hire movers for heavy items?
- What are the pickup hours? (Government buildings often have limited access windows)

A "steal" on a 10,000 lb. excavator 400 miles away is not a steal if it costs $2,000 to transport.

## 4. Start Small to Learn the System

Don't bid $15,000 on a vehicle your first time out. Start with small, low-risk items to learn how each platform works:

- Office furniture and chairs (low competition, easy pickup)
- IT equipment lots (laptops, monitors)
- Small tools and shop equipment

This teaches you the bidding process, payment timelines, and pickup procedures without financial risk. Once you understand the rhythm, scale up.

## 5. Search Across Multiple Platforms

The same type of item can sell for wildly different prices depending on which platform it's listed on and how many bidders are watching.

A set of Dell laptops on PublicSurplus might get 20 bidders and sell for $200 each. The same laptops on Municibid (smaller audience) might sell for $80 each.

This is exactly why tools like GovGrab exist — searching one platform at a time means you miss the best deals on other platforms. The fewer eyeballs on a listing, the better the deal.
