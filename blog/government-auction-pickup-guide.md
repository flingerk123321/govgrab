You won the auction. Payment cleared. Now you have 10-15 business days to get your item off government property — or you lose the item and your money. No extensions, no exceptions in most cases.

Pickup is where first-time government auction buyers get burned. The items are sold "as-is, where-is," and "where-is" can mean a military base two states away, a fenced DOT yard with limited hours, or a warehouse loading dock that's only available on Tuesdays.

Here's how to plan it right.

## Pickup Windows: Know Your Deadline

Every government auction listing includes a removal period — the window of time you have to physically remove the item from the selling agency's location after payment clears.

**Typical timelines:**
- **GSA Auctions**: 15 calendar days from payment. Extensions are rare and at the discretion of the selling agency.
- **GovDeals**: 10-15 business days, set by each selling agency. The exact window is listed on the auction page.
- **GovPlanet / IronPlanet**: Varies by auction. Check the terms — some give as few as 5 business days for on-site events.
- **PublicSurplus**: Set by the selling agency. Usually 10-15 business days.
- **Municibid**: Varies. Read the listing terms carefully.

Business days, not calendar days, is the standard — but not universal. Check whether the listing specifies business days or calendar days. A "10 business day" window over a holiday period might give you three weeks. A "10 calendar day" window is just 10 days, period.

## What Happens If You Don't Pick Up on Time

This is not a soft deadline. If you fail to remove your items within the pickup window:

- **You forfeit the item.** It goes back to the selling agency or gets re-listed.
- **You forfeit your payment.** No refund in most cases.
- **You may be banned.** Repeat no-shows can get your account suspended on platforms like GSA Auctions and GovDeals.
- **You may forfeit your removal deposit.** Some agencies require an upfront deposit specifically to ensure you show up.

Some agencies will grant a one-time extension if you call and explain the situation (weather, transport delay). But don't count on it. Treat the deadline as hard.

## Logistics Planning: Before You Bid

The time to figure out transport is before you place a bid, not after you win. Here's a checklist:

**1. Confirm the pickup location.** The listing should include an address or at least a city/state. For federal property, the facility name is usually listed (e.g., "Fort Liberty, NC" or "GSA Warehouse, Springfield, VA"). Map the distance from your location.

**2. Determine what vehicle/equipment you need.** This depends entirely on what you bought:
- **Small items** (electronics, tools, office supplies): A personal vehicle or van is fine.
- **Furniture or palletized goods**: A pickup truck, cargo van, or small box truck. Rent one from U-Haul, Penske, or Home Depot for $20-$100/day.
- **Vehicles**: Drive them home (if they run), or tow them. A tow dolly rental runs $50-$80/day. A flatbed tow truck service costs $150-$500+ depending on distance.
- **Heavy equipment**: Lowboy trailer with a CDL driver or a heavy haul company. Budget $500-$10,000+ depending on size and distance. Get quotes before you bid.
- **Large lots or palletized inventory**: A box truck or semi with a liftgate. For full truckloads, hire a freight carrier.

**3. Line up labor if needed.** Some items are too heavy or awkward for one person. If you're picking up a 2,000-lb industrial machine, you'll need help — and possibly a forklift or crane.

## Renting Trucks, Trailers, and Flatbeds

For most government auction pickups, renting a truck is the simplest option:

- **Pickup trucks**: Home Depot and Lowe's rent them for $19-$29/hour. Good for small to medium items.
- **Cargo vans and box trucks**: U-Haul, Penske, Budget. $30-$100/day depending on size. Reserve ahead — availability is limited on weekends.
- **Car hauler trailers**: U-Haul rents enclosed and open car trailers for $50-$100/day. You'll need a tow-rated vehicle.
- **Flatbed trailers**: Equipment rental yards carry them. Expect $100-$300/day. Require a hitch and tow capacity to match.
- **Lowboy/heavy haul trailers**: Not commonly available for consumer rental. Hire a heavy haul operator instead.

## Hiring Riggers and Heavy Lift Services

For items over 1,000 lbs — generators, machine tools, industrial equipment, server racks — you may need professional riggers. These are the specialists who move heavy, awkward, or fragile equipment using cranes, forklifts, jacks, and specialized dollies.

How to find them: Search "[your city] machinery movers" or "[your city] industrial rigging." Get quotes with the item's weight, dimensions, and both the pickup and delivery addresses. Expect $300-$2,000+ depending on complexity.

Important: Confirm with the selling agency whether they provide any loading assistance. Many do not. Some government facilities have forklifts available for buyer use, but this is not guaranteed and is typically the exception, not the rule. Ask the point of contact listed on the auction.

## Government Facility Access Rules

Picking up from a government facility is not like picking up from a private warehouse. Expect additional steps:

**Military bases and federal installations:**
- You'll need a valid government-issued photo ID (driver's license or passport).
- Some bases require a background check or visitor pass obtained in advance.
- Vehicles may be searched at the gate.
- You may be assigned an escort on base.
- Hours are strict — typically weekday business hours only. No weekends, no holidays.
- Foreign nationals may face additional restrictions or may not be permitted entry to certain facilities.

**State and local government facilities:**
- Generally more relaxed than federal. A driver's license and your auction receipt are usually sufficient.
- DOT yards, public works facilities, and municipal lots typically have a designated contact person for auction pickups.
- Hours vary. Some offer weekend pickup by appointment; others are weekday-only.

**In all cases:**
- Call the contact person listed on the auction before showing up. Confirm hours, gate procedures, and any access requirements.
- Bring printed copies of your payment confirmation and auction receipt.
- Bring your own tools, tie-downs, and loading equipment unless the listing says otherwise.

## Loading Dock vs. Field Pickup

Where your item is physically located on the facility affects your pickup plan:

**Loading dock**: Best case. You can back a truck up to a dock, and items can be loaded with a pallet jack or forklift. Most warehouse and office surplus is at a loading dock.

**Yard or field**: Common for vehicles, heavy equipment, and outdoor items. You'll be driving onto unpaved surfaces. Bring a truck and trailer that can handle gravel, mud, or grass. If it rained recently, plan accordingly — getting a loaded trailer stuck in a muddy DOT yard is a real scenario.

**Inside a building with no dock**: Worst case for heavy items. You may need to dolly equipment through hallways and out a standard door. Measure doorways before you show up with a 48-inch-wide machine.

## Removal Deposits

Some selling agencies require a removal deposit in addition to your payment for the item. This is a separate charge — typically $50-$500 — meant to guarantee you'll actually show up and remove what you bought.

The deposit is refunded when you complete the removal on time. If you don't pick up, you lose both the item payment and the deposit.

Not all auctions have removal deposits. When they do, it's listed in the auction terms. Read the fine print.

## Documentation Checklist

Bring these to every government auction pickup:

- Valid photo ID (government-issued)
- Printed auction receipt or winner confirmation email
- Proof of payment (wire transfer confirmation, cleared check copy)
- Bill of sale if provided by the platform
- Title application paperwork (for vehicles — some agencies handle this, some don't)
- A pen (you'll sign for the item)

For vehicles, ask the selling agency in advance what title documentation they provide. Federal agencies issue an SF-97 form (United States Government Certificate to Obtain Title to a Vehicle). State and local agencies issue standard titles or bills of sale depending on the jurisdiction.
