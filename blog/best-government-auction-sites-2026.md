There are more government auction platforms than most people realize. Federal agencies, state governments, cities, counties, school districts, and the military all sell surplus property — but they don't all use the same website.

Here are the best government auction sites in 2026, ranked by overall usefulness to buyers.

## 1. GSA Auctions

**Website**: gsaauctions.gov
**What they sell**: Federal government surplus — vehicles, IT equipment, office furniture, industrial equipment, scientific instruments, and occasionally unusual items (boats, aircraft parts, seized property).
**Buyer's premium**: 0%
**Who's selling**: Federal agencies (GSA, DOD, USPS, FBI, NASA, and others)

### Pros
- No buyer's premium. Your bid is your final price.
- Federal fleet vehicles are generally well-maintained with documented service records.
- Interesting and unique items show up regularly — things you won't find on other platforms.
- Trusted, government-run platform with no middleman.

### Cons
- Smaller inventory than GovDeals. Federal-only means fewer total listings.
- The website is functional but dated. Search and filtering could be better.
- Pickup locations are at federal facilities, which sometimes have strict access requirements and limited hours.
- Competition on popular items (vehicles, laptops) can be stiff.

**Best for**: Buyers who want zero-fee purchasing and don't mind a smaller selection. The vehicle auctions are particularly good.

## 2. GovDeals

**Website**: govdeals.com
**What they sell**: Everything. Vehicles, heavy equipment, IT equipment, office furniture, police gear, industrial tools, restaurant equipment, building materials, and more.
**Buyer's premium**: 7.5% to 12.5% (usually 12.5%)
**Who's selling**: State and local government agencies — cities, counties, school districts, transit authorities, utilities.

### Pros
- Largest inventory of any government auction platform. Thousands of active listings at all times.
- Nationwide coverage across all 50 states.
- Wide variety of categories — if a government agency owns it, GovDeals probably has one for sale.
- Email alerts for saved searches work well.

### Cons
- The 12.5% buyer's premium is the highest among major platforms. It adds up.
- Listing quality varies wildly. Some have great photos and descriptions, others are barely usable.
- High traffic means more bidders, which pushes prices up on popular items.

**Best for**: Buyers who need selection and don't mind paying the premium. If you're looking for something specific, GovDeals is the first place to check.

## 3. PublicSurplus

**Website**: publicsurplus.com
**What they sell**: Municipal surplus — vehicles, office equipment, IT hardware, police and fire equipment, parks and recreation equipment, shop tools.
**Buyer's premium**: 0% to 5% (usually 0%)
**Who's selling**: Cities, counties, school districts, and some state agencies.

### Pros
- Low or zero buyer's premiums on most auctions. This is the biggest draw.
- Clean, easy-to-use interface compared to other platforms.
- Less bidder competition than GovDeals, which keeps prices lower.

### Cons
- Smaller inventory than GovDeals.
- Geographic coverage is uneven — strong in the West, thinner in the Southeast and Northeast.
- Fewer heavy equipment and specialty vehicle listings.

**Best for**: Budget-conscious buyers and resellers where margins matter. If the same item is on PublicSurplus and GovDeals, PublicSurplus will almost always be cheaper after fees.

## 4. GovPlanet

**Website**: govplanet.com
**What they sell**: Heavy equipment, military vehicles, trucks, trailers, generators, and construction machinery. This is the Ritchie Bros. / IronPlanet platform for government and military surplus.
**Buyer's premium**: 10% to 15%
**Who's selling**: Department of Defense, federal agencies, some state and local agencies.

### Pros
- Best selection of military vehicles and heavy equipment. Humvees, MRAPs, 5-ton trucks, dozers, loaders, and more.
- Detailed IronClad Assurance inspections on many items — you get a thorough condition report with photos, which is rare in government auctions.
- Strong for construction and industrial buyers.

### Cons
- High buyer's premiums (10-15%).
- Focused almost entirely on heavy equipment and vehicles. No electronics, furniture, or general surplus.
- Prices on popular military items (like Humvees) have risen significantly as demand has grown.
- Transport logistics can be expensive for large equipment.

**Best for**: Construction companies, military vehicle enthusiasts, and heavy equipment resellers.

## 5. Municibid

**Website**: municibid.com
**What they sell**: Municipal surplus — similar to PublicSurplus and GovDeals. Vehicles, equipment, furniture, IT hardware.
**Buyer's premium**: 9%
**Who's selling**: Cities, counties, towns, school districts, and authorities.

### Pros
- Growing platform with increasing inventory, especially in the eastern United States.
- Solid coverage among smaller municipalities that aren't on the bigger platforms.
- Straightforward interface.

### Cons
- 9% buyer's premium sits in the middle — not as bad as GovDeals, but not as good as PublicSurplus.
- Smaller overall inventory than GovDeals or PublicSurplus.
- Less name recognition means some buyers overlook it, which can be an advantage (less competition).

**Best for**: Buyers in the eastern US looking for local municipal surplus. The lower profile means less bidder competition, which can offset the 9% premium.

## 6. PropertyRoom

**Website**: propertyroom.com
**What they sell**: Police-seized and recovered property — electronics, jewelry, bikes, tools, vehicles, and unclaimed personal property.
**Buyer's premium**: 14.45% (one of the highest)
**Who's selling**: Police departments and law enforcement agencies.

### Pros
- Unique inventory you won't find elsewhere. Seized electronics, jewelry, and luxury goods.
- Items often ship directly to you (unlike most government auction sites, which are pickup only).
- Good for smaller consumer items — phones, laptops, watches, power tools.

### Cons
- Very high buyer's premium at 14.45%.
- Item condition can be unpredictable. Seized property may be damaged, incomplete, or have no accessories.
- Limited vehicle selection compared to other platforms.

**Best for**: Resellers looking for electronics, jewelry, and consumer goods. The shipping option makes it more accessible than pickup-only platforms.

## 7. DLA Disposition Services (formerly DRMO)

**Website**: dla.mil/dispositionservices
**What they sell**: Military surplus — everything from clothing and MREs to truck parts, medical equipment, generators, and industrial supplies. This is the Department of Defense's surplus pipeline.
**Buyer's premium**: Varies (sales through GSA Auctions or third-party contractors)
**Who's selling**: U.S. Department of Defense.

### Pros
- Source of genuine military surplus. If you want mil-spec gear, this is the origin point.
- Some items are available through direct retail sales (not auction) at fixed prices.
- Large volume of inventory, especially for parts, supplies, and consumables.

### Cons
- The process is less user-friendly than civilian auction platforms. Navigating DLA's system takes patience.
- Many items are sold in large lots, which is better for bulk buyers than individuals.
- Demilitarized items may be modified or disabled (cutting, welding, drilling) to remove military capability.
- Pickup can be complicated — military bases have strict access protocols.

**Best for**: Bulk buyers, military surplus dealers, and anyone looking for parts, supplies, and consumables at scale.

## 8. State-Specific Surplus Programs

Many states run their own surplus sales outside the major platforms. Some examples:

- **Texas**: texasauction.com and comptroller.texas.gov/purchasing/surplus
- **California**: calauctionservices.com
- **Florida**: dms.myflorida.com/business_operations/state_surplus

### Pros
- Less competition. Many buyers don't know these exist.
- No or low buyer's premiums on some state programs.
- Good vehicle and equipment inventory from state agencies (highway departments, state police, universities).

### Cons
- You have to find and check each state separately.
- Websites and processes vary wildly in quality.
- Inventory is limited to that state's surplus.

**Best for**: Local buyers who want to avoid shipping costs and compete against fewer bidders.

## Which Platform Should You Use?

The honest answer: more than one. No single platform has everything, and prices for identical items vary across sites. A Ford F-150 on GovDeals might sell for $8,000 (plus $1,000 in premiums) while a comparable truck on PublicSurplus sells for $7,500 flat.

The trick is finding the same type of item across all platforms and buying where the true cost — bid price plus premium plus transport — is lowest.
