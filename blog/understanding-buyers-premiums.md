You won a government auction for $500. Your total cost? It might be $562.50, $545, or exactly $500 — depending on which platform you bought from. The difference is the **buyer's premium**, and if you don't account for it, it will eat into your margins.

## What Is a Buyer's Premium?

A buyer's premium is a fee charged by the auction platform on top of your winning bid. It's how these platforms make money. The seller (the government agency) doesn't pay the fee — you do.

Think of it like a service fee on concert tickets. The sticker price isn't the final price.

## Buyer's Premium by Platform

Here's what each major government surplus auction platform charges:

### GSA Auctions — 0%
The federal government's General Services Administration runs its own auction platform. Since it's run by the government itself (not a third-party company), there is **no buyer's premium**. What you bid is what you pay.

This makes GSA Auctions the most buyer-friendly platform in terms of fees. The downside: inventory is federal-only, so it's a smaller selection.

### PublicSurplus — 0% to 5%
PublicSurplus lets each selling agency set its own buyer's premium. Many agencies set it at 0%. Some charge up to 5%. The fee is listed on each auction's detail page — always check before bidding.

### Municibid — 9%
Municibid charges a flat 9% buyer's fee on all transactions. A $1,000 bid becomes $1,090.

### GovDeals — 7.5% to 12.5%
GovDeals uses a tiered premium structure. Most auctions carry a 12.5% buyer's premium. Some high-value items may have a lower rate. The exact percentage is listed on each auction page.

A $1,000 bid at 12.5% = $1,125 total.

### GovPlanet / IronPlanet — 10% to 15%
Heavy equipment auctions on GovPlanet typically carry a 10-15% buyer's premium plus any applicable taxes.

## How to Calculate True Cost

The formula is simple:

**True Cost = Winning Bid x (1 + Buyer's Premium %)**

Examples:
- $500 on GSA: $500 x 1.00 = **$500**
- $500 on Municibid: $500 x 1.09 = **$545**
- $500 on GovDeals: $500 x 1.125 = **$562.50**

That $62.50 difference matters — especially if you're reselling for profit and your margin is 20-30%.

## Why This Matters for Deal Hunting

When comparing the same type of item across platforms, the lower bid price isn't always the better deal:

- A laptop at $100 on GovDeals (12.5% fee) = $112.50 true cost
- The same laptop at $110 on GSA (0% fee) = $110.00 true cost

**The "more expensive" GSA listing is actually $2.50 cheaper.**

## Other Hidden Costs to Watch

Beyond the buyer's premium, watch for:

- **Sales tax**: Some platforms collect it, some don't. Check your state's rules.
- **Credit card processing fees**: Some platforms add 2-3% for card payments. Wire transfer or cashier's check may be cheaper.
- **Removal deposits**: Some agencies require a deposit that's refunded when you pick up the item.
- **Late removal fees**: If you don't pick up within the window (usually 10-15 business days), you may forfeit the item AND your payment.

## The Bottom Line

Always know the buyer's premium before you bid. A 12.5% premium on a $10,000 excavator is $1,250 — that's real money. Factor it into your maximum bid calculation, not after.
